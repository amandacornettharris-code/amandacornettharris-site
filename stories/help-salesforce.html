Help.Salesforce.com: From an Outdated Chat UI to Salesforce's First Agentic Conversational Experience

How a navigation-first support site — 60 million visits a year, 2 million support cases, a chat feature nobody built for — became Salesforce's live proof point for Agentforce, in under a month.

The Starting Point

Built for a different era of self-service.

Salesforce Help wasn't broken, exactly — it was just built for a different era of self-service. Every year, customers made 60 million visits, sifted through 755,000 support articles, and still opened 2 million support cases because self-service didn't get them to an answer. Chat existed on the site, but it was a secondary feature: limited in function, buried in the experience, disconnected from where customers actually needed help.1

That was the state of things through most of 2024.

The Insight — Months Before Anyone Asked

Conversation, not navigation, would be the interface.

Working with our Product VP (now SVP) on the Service Cloud design team, I identified early that conversation — not navigation — would be the primary way customers experienced and adopted Agentforce. Chat wasn't a feature to incrementally improve; it was going to be the interface Agentforce got judged by, whether we were ready or not.2

In late October 2024, I led a deep product and design sprint that produced designs, a point of view, and committed product plans for a conversation-first, agent-forward help experience — and we communicated that plan widely before Salesforce's own internal Agentforce Kick-Off.2

The Turn — November 5, 2024

Customer Zero, and five days on the clock.

At Agentforce Kick-Off (AKO), Marc Benioff declared that Help.Salesforce.com would become Salesforce's live "Customer Zero" showcase — the company's own proof point for Agentforce, in front of customers and the market.2

The parameters were blunt: redesign the UI, prioritize Customer Zero — and you've got 5 days to lock design and specs.3 A cross-functional team formed almost overnight: the MIAW (Messaging In-App & Web) team and the Customer Success team, previously working in separate lanes, became — in the words of the project retro — "one team, overnight."4 I led design for that 15-person founding group.5

"Integrate Agentforce into Salesforce Help AND make self-service easier for customers experiencing an issue."
Leadership's ask, verbatim — Project retrospective
The Build — 5 Days to Spec, 1 Month to Ship

Deliberately simple tooling. Work done in the open.

The team kept tooling deliberately simple: Figma for design specs, a shared Google Sheet ("Chat x Help Prioritization") to sequence feature work, and a single Slack channel as "our everything place" — where design, product, and engineering worked in the open, in real time.6

I described my own role across three levels, reflected in the project retrospective:7

As an individual
Understand the technology, form an informed opinion, improve the end-to-end experience anywhere I could — in both breadth and depth.
As a people leader
Empower the team's Messaging Lead Designer, unblock the critical path, check titles at the door and work as a coalition.
As an enterprise influencer
Communicate up, down, and sideways; find the smartest people and get out of their way; forge partnerships across orgs; own executive summary and escalation.

From concept to shipped product, in under a month — ahead of Investor Day.2

The Outcome

A search box became a conversation.

The rebuilt experience replaced a static "how can we help?" search box with a conversational Agentforce interface, front and center on the homepage.8

Measured cumulatively, the rebuilt experience now drives 82% self-service resolution across 2.2M+ AI-powered interactions — lifetime figures independently confirmed in Bernard Slowey's June all-hands.10

The underlying strategy and framework — reframing the experience around five mindset shifts (navigation→conversation, content management→knowledge engineering, siloed specialist→cross-functional coalition, fixed UI→flexible engagement, building features→designing outcomes) — reached customers directly at TDX. I co-authored that presentation for the team to deliver on stage; Emily Winslow and Nimma Bhusri presented it.11

What Came Next

The work didn't stop at launch.

Amanda continued as a named co-author on Salesforce's forward-looking roadmap for the next generation of chat and conversational experiences.*

Reflection

A bet on how AI products actually get adopted.

This wasn't a redesign. It was a bet on how AI products actually get adopted — made months before the moment demanded it, then executed at founding-team speed once it did. Frictionless self-service activation, rapid iteration, and treating a help center as a growth engine rather than a cost center: the same instincts that drive product-led growth in consumer and B2B SaaS, applied here to reshape how millions of enterprise customers experienced AI for the first time.
